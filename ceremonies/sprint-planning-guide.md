# Sprint Planning Guide  
Tips for running an effective Agile Sprint Planning session.

> Based on the original article:  
> https://ericspink.medium.com/tips-for-an-effective-agile-sprint-planning-bd4726af889c

Sprints are the engine of Scrum — they turn ideas into real value. But by the time a team reaches Sprint Planning, they’ve already pushed through finishing stories, the Sprint Review, and the Retrospective. Energy is low, and attention is scattered. That’s exactly why Sprint Planning needs to be as effective and focused as possible.

This guide captures practical tips to help teams plan with clarity and confidence.

---

## 🧭 What Sprint Planning Is  
The Scrum Guide describes Sprint Planning as the event that *“initiates the Sprint by laying out the work to be performed”* and emphasizes that the plan is created collaboratively by the entire Scrum Team. 

Sprint Planning should answer three core questions:
- Why is this Sprint valuable?  
- What can be done this Sprint?  
- How will the chosen work get done?

---

## 📊 Start by Reviewing Key Agile Metrics  
Reviewing metrics early gives the team context, confidence, and predictability. Several metrics worth discussing:

### **Average Velocity**  
Shows how many story points the team typically completes. Helps set a realistic planning target.

### **Velocity Variance**  
Reveals how last sprint’s velocity compares to the average. Useful for gauging confidence in the upcoming plan.

### **Committed vs. Completed**  
Only bring this up if there’s a trend of over‑ or under‑commitment in the last few sprints. It helps reinforce stability and predictability.

### **Automated Test Coverage**  
If coverage is low, the team may need to plan time to improve it. This metric helps teams understand quality risks early.

These metrics give the team a grounded starting point before diving into the details.

---

## 🗓️ Determine Upcoming Sprint Capacity  
Capacity changes every sprint — vacations, holidays, workshops, and PTO all matter. Your article stresses the importance of asking the team who will be away and for how long, and reminding everyone of upcoming events.

A shared team calendar is a powerful tool here. It helps everyone see availability at a glance and reduces surprises.

---

## 🔁 Bring Forward Retrospective Action Items  
A good Retrospective produces meaningful action items — and those items require time and effort.

- Adding action items as user stories  
- Placing them at the top of the Sprint Backlog  
- Making them visible during planning  

This ensures the team doesn’t forget them and can plan around them intentionally.

---

## 🔄 Address Carried‑Over Stories  
Stories carry over — even for mature teams.

1. **Ask how much effort remains**  
   Don’t resize the story; simply note the remaining effort and factor it into planning.

2. **Re‑evaluate priority**  
   If priorities have shifted, it may be appropriate to move the story back to the Product Backlog.  
   When doing so, ensure notes are added about what’s done, where the code lives, and what’s left.

This prevents confusion and preserves context.

---

## 🏁 Ensure Each Story Meets the Definition of Ready  
A great example of a vague story (“We need to fix the report…”) to show how missing details can derail a sprint.

A mature team uses a Definition of Ready (DoR) to avoid this. During planning:

- Confirm each story meets the DoR  
- Push back on stories that aren’t ready  
- Avoid refining during planning unless absolutely necessary  

If many stories fail the DoR, it’s a sign that backlog refinement needs improvement.

---

## 🚫 Don’t Combine Refinement with Sprint Planning  
A common agile anti‑pattern: combining Refinement and Planning to “reduce meetings.”

- Planning becomes longer and more exhausting  
- Stories remain unclear  
- Requirements get gathered mid‑sprint  
- Output quality drops  

Instead, address the root cause: ineffective ceremonies, poor backlog health, or unnecessary meetings elsewhere.

---

## 🎯 Keep Conversations Focused  
Sprint Planning often derails when the team dives too deep into solutioning.

- Allowing some discussion  
- Stepping in when the conversation gets too detailed  
- Reminding the team that Planning is about starting the sprint, not designing every solution  

If deep dives keep happening, it may indicate:
- Poor refinement  
- Missing information  
- A team habit of going off‑topic  

Bring these patterns to the Retrospective.

---

## ⏲ Plan Slack Into the Sprint  
Life is unpredictable — and so are sprints. You recommend planning roughly **80%** of the team’s capacity, leaving **20%** for:

- Production bugs  
- Sick days  
- Unexpected complexity  

If nothing unexpected happens, that slack becomes time for learning, small improvements, or early work on future items.

---

## 🔢 Order Stories by Priority  
Just like the Product Backlog, the Sprint Backlog should be ordered. Benefits include:

- Developers know what to pick up next  
- Leadership sees the sprint’s focus  
- The team can easily spot if high‑priority items are at risk  

This ordering helps the team swarm effectively when needed.

---

## 👍 Confirm the Sprint Goal Is Achievable  
Sprint planning is a collaborative effort:

- PO brings priorities  
- Scrum Master brings metrics  
- Tech Lead brings technical considerations  
- Developers bring feasibility  

Before closing planning, step back and ask:

- Does the Sprint Backlog align with the Sprint Goal?  
- Are there red flags (too many large stories, dependencies, misaligned items)?  
- Does the commitment match historical velocity and capacity?  
- Does the team feel confident?

This final check builds shared ownership.

---

## 🏁 Conclusion  
Sprint Planning kicks off the sprint and sets the tone for everything that follows. By applying these tips — reviewing metrics, checking capacity, honoring action items, ensuring readiness, staying focused, and validating the plan — teams create a strong foundation for predictable, high‑quality delivery.
