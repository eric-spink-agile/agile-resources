# Sprint Review Guide  
Tips for running an effective Agile Sprint Review.

> Based on the original article:  
> https://ericspink.medium.com/tips-for-an-effective-agile-sprint-review-d77afca45c5d

The team has worked hard, delivered new value, and now it’s time to show it. But a Sprint Review is more than a demo — it’s a chance to gather feedback, answer questions, and help stakeholders understand the direction of the product. A well‑run Sprint Review strengthens alignment and improves future decisions.

This guide captures practical tips to help you run a focused, valuable Sprint Review.

---

## 🧭 What a Sprint Review Is  
The Scrum Guide states: *“The purpose of the Sprint Review is to inspect the outcome of the Sprint and determine future adaptations.”* 

It’s a working session — not just a presentation — where the Scrum Team and stakeholders:

- Review what was accomplished  
- Discuss what changed in the environment  
- Explore what to do next  
- Adjust the Product Backlog if needed  

The Sprint Review happens near the end of the Sprint and is timeboxed (up to four hours for a one‑month Sprint).

---

## 📅 Schedule It at the End of the Sprint — and Keep It Consistent  
Finding time for a large group is difficult. Your article emphasizes that the simplest solution is to **book the Sprint Review as a recurring meeting**. This allows stakeholders to plan around it and reduces scheduling chaos.

Holding the Sprint Review at the end of the Sprint is essential — feedback gathered here can influence Sprint Planning immediately afterward.

---

## 🎯 Present for the Right Audience  
Stakeholders are the primary audience. When you speak their language, they understand the work better and are more likely to give meaningful feedback.

- Technical details are for the team  
- The Sprint Review is for stakeholders  
- Speak in terms they understand  
- Focus on value, outcomes, and impact  

When stakeholders understand what they’re seeing, they ask better questions and provide better guidance.

---

## 🧩 Use a Template  
A template keeps the focus on content rather than slide design. It ensures:

- Consistency across Sprint Reviews  
- Predictable structure for stakeholders  
- Faster preparation for the team  
- A standard that can be shared across teams  

Templates help keep the ceremony clean, professional, and easy to follow.

---

## 📝 Make a Quick Plan With the Team  
A messy Sprint Review distracts from the work. You recommend spending **15 minutes** a day or two before the Review to:

- Fill out the template  
- List what’s Done and In‑Progress  
- Identify challenges  
- Prepare who will demo what  
- Build a simple agenda  

This small investment pays off in clarity and stakeholder confidence.

---

## 🎥 Record the Sprint Review  
You shared a great story about a company that repeated the demo multiple times because stakeholders arrived at different times. Recording solves this problem.

Recording helps because:

- Stakeholders can watch later  
- You avoid repeating the session  
- You can share the link afterward  
- More people see the work  
- You can still gather feedback from those who missed it  

Most meeting tools can auto‑record — turn it on.

---

## 📣 Share the Agenda Ahead of Time  
Not everyone needs to attend every Sprint Review. Sharing the agenda early helps:

- Stakeholders know what will be shown  
- They can invite relevant people  
- Users interested in specific features can join  
- Others can skip if the content isn’t relevant  

This respects everyone’s time and increases targeted feedback.

---

## 📋 Summarize Done and In‑Progress Stories  
Transparency is a pillar of Scrum. Your article quotes the Scrum Guide: *“Artifacts that have low transparency can lead to decisions that diminish value and increase risk.”* 

Summarizing Done and In‑Progress stories:

- Shows the real state of the product  
- Helps stakeholders understand effort  
- Makes pivot decisions easier  
- Provides context for the demo  

Keep it quick — a simple list at the start works well.

---

## 🧱 Share Challenges to Get Support  
Sprint Reviews are a great place to highlight challenges that leadership can help with. You recommend:

- Sharing blockers the team faced  
- Mentioning how the team solved some issues  
- Calling out challenges leadership can influence  
- Highlighting repeated patterns  

Stakeholders want the team to succeed — but they can only help if they know what’s happening.

---

## ⏳ Use the 8‑Second Rule  
After each section — a demo, a list of stories, or a challenge — pause for **eight seconds**.

This gives stakeholders time to:

- Think  
- Form questions  
- Offer feedback  

Without this pause, teams rush ahead and miss valuable input.

---

## 🕒 Keep It Focused and Respect Stakeholder Time  
Sprint Reviews often derail when:

- Demos go too deep  
- Team members repeat themselves  
- Stakeholders ask unrelated questions  

Your guidance:

- Keep demos high‑level  
- Step in when conversations drift  
- Suggest offline follow‑ups  
- Protect the time of the group  

When the Review stays focused, people stay engaged.

---

## 🛠️ Show Technical Work in a Non‑Technical Way  
Backend work matters — but showing code to non‑technical stakeholders doesn’t help. Instead:

- Explain the user story or feature it supports  
- Describe the value or impact  
- Share diagrams or high‑level documentation  
- Give measurable improvements (e.g., faster load times)  

This helps stakeholders appreciate the work and provide relevant feedback.

---

## 🗣️ Ask for Feedback About the Sprint Review Itself  
The Sprint Review is for stakeholders — so ask them how to improve it.

- Gathering feedback after a few Sprint Reviews  
- Meeting with key stakeholders individually  
- Using a feedback form for larger groups  
- Adjusting the format based on what brings value  

Not every request should be included — only what supports the purpose of the Sprint Review.

---

## 🏁 Conclusion  
A strong Sprint Review gives stakeholders visibility, invites meaningful feedback, and helps the team improve the product. By applying these tips — consistent scheduling, clear agendas, focused demos, transparency, and stakeholder‑centered communication — you create a Review that delivers real value.
